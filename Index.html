Simon Says
One student is 'Simon' and stands in front of the class. The class only follows a command if it begins with 'Simon says…'. Anyone who follows a command without that prefix is out. The last student remaining becomes the next Simon.
Example: Simon says: 'Touch your nose!' → everyone touches their nose. Then: 'Clap your hands!' (no 'Simon says') → anyone who claps is out.

Do as I Say, Not as I Do
Teacher gives a command but deliberately does a DIFFERENT action at the same time. Students must follow what is SAID, not what they see the teacher do.
Example: Teacher says 'Touch your knees' but touches their own ears. Students who copy the action (touching ears) instead of listening to the words are out.

Do What I Said, Not What I Say
Students must always perform the PREVIOUS command — not the one just given. Each new command is effectively a one-step delay. A harder variation of Simon Says.
Example: Leader: 'Stand on one foot!' → students do nothing (no previous command yet). Leader: 'Hop!' → students stand on one foot. Leader: 'Clap!' → students hop.

Follow the Leader
Students line up behind the teacher and walk around the classroom. The teacher performs an action and shouts its name. Students copy the action and repeat the word aloud together.
Example: Teacher jumps and shouts 'Jump!' → everyone jumps and says 'Jump!' Teacher waves and says 'Wave!' → everyone waves and says 'Wave!'

Slam
Flashcards or small objects are placed in the center of a circle of students. Everyone keeps their hands on their heads. Teacher calls a word — students race to be first to touch (slam) the correct item. Most items collected wins.
Example: Cards on the floor: cat, dog, bird, fish. Teacher says 'bird!' → fastest student to slam the bird card keeps it. Continue until all cards are claimed.

Rope Jump

Eleven
Students sit in a circle and count from 1 upward, one number per student going around the circle. The student who says '11' is out. Remaining students switch seats and start again from 1. The last student remaining wins.
Example: Ali:1 → Sara:2 → Reza:3 → … → whoever says 11 sits down. Seats are shuffled and a new round begins from 1 again.

Line True or False
A line of tape divides the room into a 'True' side and a 'False' side. Teacher holds up a flashcard and says a word. If the word MATCHES the card, students jump to True. If it doesn't match, they jump to False. Wrong choice = sit out.
Example: Teacher holds up a cat picture and says 'Cat!' → True side. Teacher holds the cat picture and says 'Dog!' → False side. Students who jump the wrong way are eliminated.

Number Codes
Prepare sets of number cards (0–9) in a box for each student. Teacher calls out a sequence of digits aloud. Students take out their number cards and arrange them in order. At the end, check if everyone's sequence matches what was said.
Example: Teacher calls: '3 – 7 – 1 – 5 – 9.' Students arrange: 3, 7, 1, 5, 9. Check together. Next: '0 – 8 – 4 – 2 – 6'. Good practice for phone numbers and dates.

Bingo
Students draw a grid (e.g. 4×4) and fill each square with a number, letter, or word from a given list. Teacher randomly calls items one by one. Students cross off matches. First to complete a line — or the full card — shouts 'Bingo!' and wins.
Example: Topic: numbers 1–20. Students fill a 4×4 grid with any 16 numbers. Teacher calls '7… 13… 4… 19…' Students cross them off. First to cross off a complete row, column, or diagonal shouts Bingo!

Kids
Weather Concentration
Two identical sets of vocabulary flashcards (e.g. sunny, rainy, cloudy, snowy…) are shuffled and placed face-down in a grid on a table. Students take turns flipping two cards. If they match, the student keeps the pair and goes again. Most pairs wins.
Example: Student flips 'sunny' and 'rainy' → no match, turn back over. Next student flips 'cloudy' and 'cloudy' → match! Keeps the pair and takes another turn.

Vocabulary Tunnel
Create a tunnel for students to crawl through (using desks pushed together or students forming an arch). The teacher stands at one end holding a flashcard. Before a student can crawl through, they must correctly say the word shown on the card. Correct answer = through the tunnel! Wrong answer = try again. Works as a fun physical reward for correct vocabulary recall.
Example: Tunnel made from 3 desks. Teacher holds 'hospital' at the entrance. Ali: 'Hospital!' Correct → crawls through. Teacher holds 'police station'. Sara: 'Library?' Wrong → steps aside. Sara: 'Police station!' Correct → crawls through excitedly.

Window Game
This activity requires a ground-floor classroom window. The teacher goes outside and silently mouths a flashcard word through the glass without making any sound. Students inside watch and try to lip-read the word. The student who first correctly identifies what the teacher is saying gets to go outside for the next turn. Model the activity carefully before students take turns.
Example: Teacher goes outside → mouths 'elephant' slowly and repeatedly through the window. Students watch: 'Is it... elephant? Elephant!' First student to correctly call it out gets to go outside and silently mouth their own flashcard word to the class.

Flashcard
Zoo Game
Assign each student an animal flashcard. Students sit in different areas of the classroom, each 'being' their assigned animal. The teacher walks around greeting and asking questions to individual students. Each student may only respond using their animal's sound — no words allowed! A fun, low-pressure activity for young learners that associates vocabulary with sounds and contexts.
Example: Ali is a cow, Sara is a lion, Reza is a dog. Teacher: 'Hello, Ali, how are you?' Ali: 'Moo! Moo!' Teacher: 'What's your name, Sara?' Sara: 'Roar!' Teacher: 'Are you happy, Reza?' Reza: 'Woof-woof!' Class can guess which animal each student represents.

Action Race
Split the class into two teams. Each team sits in a line with a chair beside them and another chair at the far end of the room. One student from each team stands next to their chair. The teacher calls out an action (e.g. 'Jump!'). Both students must perform that action all the way to the chair at the other end of the room and back, then sit down and say 'I can jump!' The first student to sit down and say the sentence correctly wins a point for their team.
Example: Teacher: 'Hop!' → both students hop to the far chair and back, sit down and say 'I can hop!' Teacher: 'Clap!' → both clap their way across and back: 'I can clap!' First back wins the point.

Art Gallery
Draw a grid of squares on the board — one square per student. Each student writes their name above their square. The teacher calls out a word (simple nouns, verb structures, or adjectives) and every student draws it in their own square simultaneously. The teacher reviews each drawing and assigns a score. The student with the highest total score at the end wins. Works well for nouns ('dog', 'bookcase'), verb structures ('a man running'), or adjectives ('a big elephant').
Example: Teacher: 'Draw an angry lion!' → all students draw simultaneously in their squares. Teacher walks around scoring 1–5 per drawing. Teacher: 'Draw a man eating cake!' → students draw again. Highest cumulative score wins.

Attention

Blind Toss
Place a mat on the floor with numbers written on it, and a flashcard (target vocabulary word) placed at each number. Students take turns being blindfolded and tossing a beanbag onto the mat. Whatever number the beanbag lands on, the student must say the vocabulary word on that square the same number of times as the number indicates, earning that many points. For example, landing on number 4 with the word 'dog' = say 'Dog!' four times = 4 points. Most points wins.

Count-Off
Students stand in a circle or in lines (requires at least 10 students). The teacher gestures to one student who says '1.' The counting continues around the circle or line, each student saying the next number up to 20, then restarting from 1. For a higher level, the teacher points to students at random rather than in order. If a student is too slow or says the wrong number, they are eliminated. The last student standing wins.
Example: Ali: '1', Sara: '2', Reza: '3', … up to 20, then restart. Higher level: teacher points randomly → Ali: '1' → teacher skips to Leila: '2' → back to Reza: '3'. If Reza says '4' when it should be '3', they're out.

Days / Months March
You need a fairly long classroom with space to march. The teacher stands at one end and students line up alongside. Everyone marches together as the teacher calls out the days of the week (or months of the year) in order; students repeat each one aloud. At random moments, the teacher suddenly shouts 'Stop!' — everyone must freeze and be exactly in line with the teacher. Anyone out of line is ordered back. Continue marching and turn around at the end of the room. Do it slowly first, then briskly, then at a run. After a few sessions, students chant independently without the teacher leading.
Example: Teacher: 'January!' Students: 'January!' Teacher: 'February!' Students: 'February!'… Teacher suddenly: 'Stop!' → everyone freezes. Reza is ahead → 'Back in line, Reza!' Resume: 'March!' Students: 'March!' Turn around, repeat at faster pace.

Label It
Label everything in the classroom with index cards showing the item's name. On Day 1, teacher reads each label aloud while students point to the item and repeat. On Day 2, remove the cards and go through them one at a time as a class, placing each on the correct object together. On Day 3, students independently label whatever they can on their own. Later, deliberately put labels on the WRONG objects — students must find all mistakes and correct them. Can be adapted to any noun-based vocabulary list.
Example: Day 3: Label 'DOOR' is placed on the window and 'WINDOW' is on the door. Students walk around: 'This is wrong — door is here!' and swap them. 'BOARD' is on the desk → 'No, the board is there!' Students fix all wrong labels.

Odd One Out
The teacher writes three or four words on the board. Students must identify and circle the word that does not belong to the same category as the others, and explain why. Can be played as a competition (first to raise hand correctly wins a point) or as a quiet thinking activity. Works well for category awareness, vocabulary review, and critical thinking.
Example: Board: 'cat — horse — cake — bird'. Odd one out: 'cake' (the others are animals). Board: 'red — chair — blue — green' → odd one out: 'chair' (not a color). Board: 'swim — run — jump — desk' → 'desk' (not a verb).

Preposition Treasure Hunt
Roll a small piece of sticky tack (e.g. Blue Tack) into a ball and demonstrate: give it to a student and ask them to hide it somewhere tricky (on the underside of a desk, on the wall behind a curtain, etc.) while you leave the room. Come back in and find it by asking only Yes/No questions using prepositions of location: 'Is it on a desk?', 'Is it under something?', 'Is it near the door?', etc. Once found, have students take over both the hiding and the questioning roles. Example: Student hides sticky tack on the underside of a chair. Teacher returns: 'Is it on the wall?' No. 'Is it near the front?' Yes. 'Is it under something?' Yes. 'Is it under a chair?' Yes. 'Is it on the underside of this chair?' Yes! Found it!

Rhythmic Reading
Choose a reading passage (one page for basic texts, one paragraph for advanced). The teacher establishes a rhythm by clapping or tapping on the desk. One student at a time reads one sentence aloud — exactly on the beat, at the correct pace, and with correct pronunciation. The next student begins their sentence immediately as the previous one ends. Any student who misses the beat, stumbles, or mispronounces loses a 'life' or is eliminated. Eliminated students help keep the beat. Can be played as a team game.

Squeeze
Divide students into two teams facing each other across a table. Students on each team hold hands in a chain — except for the two students at the far end who each reach toward a small object (e.g. a koosh ball or eraser) in the middle. The two students closest to the teacher watch with eyes open; everyone else closes their eyes. Teacher flips a coin. If it lands HEADS, the watching student squeezes the next hand — the squeeze travels down the chain. The student at the end grabs the object. If it lands TAILS, nothing happens. Accidentally grabbing for tails loses a point.
Example: Teacher flips coin → Heads! Team A's watcher squeezes → squeeze travels: S1→S2→S3→S4 who grabs the object first → Team A wins the point. Line rotates. Next flip → Tails → no squeeze. Team B's student accidentally grabs → Team A gets a point.

Uhm Game
One student at a time is given a topic — it can be concrete (a pencil, a tree) or abstract (freedom, luck). The student must speak about that subject for as long as possible without pausing, hesitating, or using filler words such as 'um', 'uh', 'like', or 'you know.'
